# **COUNSEL Section**

**\[The Doctor has finished the well check and is ready to introduce the HPV vaccine.\]** 

**COACH PROMPT FOR THIS SECTION:**  
*Please begin by giving your Counsel statement. Remember to include the key elements from the C-LEAR pocket card: Age of child; Prevents six types of cancer; Safe; Recommended today; and Return for second dose.*

**Clinician, MD:** So, Riley looks healthy and doesn't have any major problems from the physical today. But it looks like she is due for the HPV vaccine. This is a vaccine recommended for children starting at age 9 that helps prevent six types of cancer. I recommend they get this vaccine today and then come back in 6 to 12 months to get the second dose.

## **COACH FEEDBACK FOR THIS SECTION:**

*I appreciate the effort you put into that. You included 4 out of the 5 elements: The HPV vaccine is recommended for 10 year olds, it prevents against six types of cancer, and the child will come back in 6 months for a second dose. Next time, you might try to include that the vaccine is safe, which is an important component of the Counsel statement.*

**COACH PROMPT FOR THIS SECTION:**  
*Now, listen to the parent’s concern. Use an active Listening skill, either restating or exploring their concern.* 

**ANNE PALMER:**  Does she really need that one? Because she's only 10\.

Next time, try pausing after the parent’s concern and using a Listen skill, such as Explore: “Tell me more about your concerns.” If you had used a Listen skill, you would have discovered that the parent’s primary concern was that since the child isn’t having sex, the parent didn’t understand why they would need the vaccine.*

# **EMPATHIZE Section**

**COACH PROMPT FOR THIS SECTION:**  
*Empathy sets the foundation for the rest of the conversation and should come before answering the parent’s question. After you hear the parent’s next statement, use an Empathy  skill that acknowledges, normalizes, or validates the concern. Do not answer the question yet.*

**ANNE PALMER:**  Well, she’s not having sex yet. I don’t see why she needs to get this now.

**Clinician, MD:** I can understand your concern for giving the vaccine at a young age.

## **COACH FEEDBACK FOR THIS SECTION: (Empathize)**

*I appreciate your willingness to work through this. When the parent responded, you followed up with an Empathy skill (Acknowledge) before answering the parent’s question.*

# **ANSWER-RECOMMEND Section**

**COACH PROMPT FOR THIS SECTION:**  
*Now, answer the parent’s question or concern about why their child needs the vaccine if they aren’t having sex yet, using the FAQ guide as a reference. Remember to end your answer with a strong Recommendation. *

**Clinician, MD:** We're not thinking of them having sex at this point, but when we administer the vaccine early, it helps protect them before they are ever exposed to the virus. So she will be protected from a younger age when she decides at some point to have any sexual activity.

Also, if she gets it at a young age, the only thing she will be needing is 2 shots from that vaccine. If we administer one today, the second dose would be given in 6 to 12 months. But if she gets a bit older, she will be needing 3 doses of the vaccine. So getting it a little bit younger, it's also a little bit of an advantage there.

**ANNE PALMER:**  Hmm… I guess I’ll think about it.

## **COACH FEEDBACK (Answer with no Recommend)**

*You clearly put thought into how you approached this. Your Answer included both elements for why we recommend giving the HPV vaccine as young as age 9: That it works better at a younger age and that it protects the child even before they are exposed to the virus through sex.* 

*Next time, try using a strong Recommendation at the end of your Answer: “The HPV vaccine protects your child even before they are exposed to the virus through sex, and it works better at a younger age (Answer), that’s why we strongly recommend she gets it (Recommend).” If you had ended with a strong Recommendation, the parent might have agreed to the vaccine.*
